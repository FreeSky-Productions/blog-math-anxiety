A deer in headlights

"I have said in the past that it is not clear whether intelligence has any long-term survival value - bacteria multiply and flourish without it."

Something rare happened at work recently. I was running a Math drop-in session for an event called "Long Night Against Procrastination", and there were about six or eight students sat a table near me. Naturally, I went up to the group to introduce myself and welcome them to the drop-in. Turns out, this group was just waiting for pizza to be served, and their college program had no math courses. However, they mentioned that they were all fascinated by math, and asked me give them a math problem to solve while they waited for the pizza.

    Intrigued, I inquired about their level of proficiency in math. They mentioned that they studied mathematics at the intermediate level. Astute readers may have noted my use of the definite article here - since this verbiage alludes to a particular education system - so please comment below if you are familiar with what "intermediate level" means. Anyways, back to the story. I asked them to solve the following relatively challenging calculus problem:

`\int ( \sin x + \cos x )^{2} dx`.

It took some good old-fashioned team-work, perseverance, and a little guidance from yours truly, but the problem was solved within 30 minutes. Pizza still wasn't here. To my astonishment, this group of students loved working on the problem so much that they asked me for a second problem!

This time they requested a Statistics problem as they felt more comfortable with that. In the fashion of a true Bond villain, I gave them a classic problem involving conditional probability:

Suppose in an epidemic, it is known that about 5% of the population is sick. Scientists have developed a rapid test which gives 3% false positive and 2% false negative results. Given an individual has tested negative, what is the probability that they are sick?

Of course the students were stumped. This was not an easy problem. Nevertheless, they were keen to understand how to work through the problem. Once I explained the idea of tree-diagrams in conditional probability, they were soon able calculate marginal probabilities. After a brief demonstration of Bayes' rule using the tree-diagram (I never wrote down the formula for Bayes' rule for these students), they were able to get the solution. Their excitement was pulpable. Alas, pizza arrived soon after, so math took the backseat. Nevertheless, they stayed back to shake my hands after finishing their meal.

What I saw in those students that day was love and joy from discovering mathematics. Remember, these students have not taken, and will not be taking, any college math courses. It was unusual that they would want to solve math problems, and downright improbable that they would enjoy the process. This experience brought me an exuberance that I haven't felt in quite some time. Wouldn't it be lovely if all of my engagements were so positive?
